# Silicon-Photonic-Logic-Gate

Artificial neural networks and photonic devices are both major areas of interest because they offer the possibility of fast, trainable, and highly parallel information processing. Although artificial neural networks and machine learning are now mature technologies, they have been implemented mainly on electronic hardware. At the same time, the growing demand for high-speed data processing and communication continues to highlight the bandwidth and speed limitations of purely electronic signals. For this reason, it is important to investigate whether neural-network operations can be realized using optical devices. 
In an artificial neural network, the linear unit is primarily the matrix multiplication operation, and this can be implemented in the optical domain in several ways. One common approach uses optical interference in Mach–Zehnder interferometers, while other photonic matrix-processing architectures can also be used, including directional-coupler-based structures such as those demonstrated by Feldmann et al. [1]. The nonlinear activation function, however, has generally been explored in two forms. The first is purely all-optical activation, in which the signal remains in the optical domain through the nonlinear operation. The second is electro-optic activation, in which part or all of the optical signal is converted into an electrical signal, the nonlinear function is applied, and the result is then used to modulate or regenerate the optical output. Both approaches are actively studied, although they are not equally mature. In principle, a fully all-optical neural network could provide lower latency and lower energy consumption.
Shi et al. used a germanium-silicon photodiode in which nonlinear activation and monitoring are combined in the same device [2]. In their structure, part of the Ge region has no electrode. In that electrode-free region, photogenerated carriers accumulate instead of being swept out quickly. This carrier accumulation increases free-carrier absorption (FCA) in the Ge film, which makes the output optical power depend nonlinearly on the input optical power. At the same time, the collected carriers provide an electrical monitoring signal. Therefore, the device enables all-optical nonlinear activation together with non-intrusive monitoring in the same structure.
This work investigates, at the system level, whether a silicon-photonic NAND gate built from a linear optical unit and an optical nonlinear activation block can be realized and then further studied by cascading the NAND gate to examine XOR realization. One advantage of the NAND gate is that the NAND function is linearly separable, unlike XOR and XNOR. Another advantage is that the NAND gate can be cascaded to realize all other logic gates, as Floyd explained in his textbook [3].
